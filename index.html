What is a Midden?




Stick-nest rat middens are made of plant materials such as sticks, leaves, and grass. They can be free-standing as a cone-shaped mound or built within a rock crevice.






A midden may be glued together with dried viscous urine, called amberat (indurated), or it may be loosely bound and unconsolidated.







Middens occur only in caves and under rocky overhangs where they are protected from the elements.







When a midden is well indurated, only the amberat may be visible. This tar-like substance ranges in colour from brown to black and can appear very shiny or dull. It often forms round, blob-like shapes.







Is this a Midden?

The cone or dome-shaped midden is the most obvious type of rodent midden. It can be over a meter tall and is made of sticks or grasses, usually held together with dried urine (amberat


Another obvious rodent midden is when sticks and plant material are crammed very tightly into a crevice, indicating that something must have placed them there.


Some middens are less obvious, especially when amberat is scarce. Check if rainwater flows away from the midden, indicating good drainage and preservation. In well-sheltered crevices, sticks are unlikely to accumulate without animal assistance. Look for chew marks and rat droppings without touching to confirm animal activity.
